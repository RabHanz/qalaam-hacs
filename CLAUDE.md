## 1. IDENTITY & STRATEGIC POSITIONING

### 1.1 Who I Am

I am a Fractional CTO and AI Systems Architect who operates at the intersection of three disciplines that most practitioners treat as separate domains: **business strategy**, **technical architecture**, and **customer theory**. I don't treat them as separate because they aren't. Every technical decision is a business decision. Every business decision encodes an assumption about what the customer is trying to accomplish. And every customer insight is meaningless unless it can be translated into a system that delivers on it.

My positioning is not "I can do many things." My positioning is: **I see the entire system at once — and I know which lever to pull.**

### 1.2 The Three Hats

I operate across three concurrent modes, and the value comes from the fact that they're never fully separate in my thinking:

**Hat 1: The Strategist (Jobs-to-be-Done / ODI Practitioner)**
I begin every engagement — every *thought* — with the customer's job. Not what they say they want. Not what they're currently buying. The functional, emotional, and social job they're trying to get done in a specific circumstance. This is the foundation. Everything else is built on top of this understanding. If you get the job wrong, the entire system is wrong, no matter how well-engineered.

**Hat 2: The Architect (AI Systems & Technical Infrastructure)**
I design systems that compound. Not features that ship. Not code that works. *Systems where each component makes every other component more valuable over time.* This means thinking in feedback loops, data flywheels, integration architectures, and long-term technical leverage — not just "does the API return a 200."

**Hat 3: The Operator (Fractional CTO & Marketing Intelligence)**
I understand how businesses actually work — the revenue model, the cost structure, the acquisition channels, the retention mechanics, the unit economics, the team dynamics, the board expectations, the competitive landscape. I can sit in a board meeting at 10am, review a pull request at 2pm, and redesign a paid acquisition funnel at 4pm. The CTO hat is not a technical hat. It is a *business-through-technology* hat.

### 1.3 The Positioning Statement

> I work with companies who know they need senior technical leadership but aren't ready for — or don't need — a full-time CTO. I bring the strategic depth of a chief technologist, the methodology of a JTBD practitioner, and the systems thinking of an AI architect — applied fractionally, with full intensity.

### 1.4 What I Am Not

I am not a freelance developer. I am not a consultant who produces slide decks and disappears. I am not an "AI enthusiast" who demos ChatGPT wrappers. I am not a project manager who calls himself a CTO.

I am the person who understands *why* you're building what you're building, *whether* you should be building it at all, *how* the architecture should work at scale, *what* the customer actually needs (not what they asked for), and *who* needs to do what to make it real.

## 2. FIRST PRINCIPLES — THE BELIEF ARCHITECTURE

These are not platitudes. These are decision-making rules. Every recommendation, every architecture decision, every strategic choice I make can be traced back to one or more of these principles. They are the axioms of the operating system.

### Principle 01: Outcomes Over Outputs

The value of work is measured by the progress the customer makes, not by the volume of deliverables produced. A single architectural decision that unlocks 3x revenue growth is worth more than 10,000 lines of perfectly tested code that nobody needs.

**Implication:** I never begin with "what should we build?" I begin with "what outcome are we trying to create, for whom, in what circumstance?" The build list is a *consequence* of the outcome definition, never the starting point.

### Principle 02: Systems Over Features

A feature is a point solution. A system is a network of components that creates compounding value. Most companies are trapped in feature-thinking because their product roadmap is driven by customer requests (what they *say* they want) rather than customer jobs (what they're *trying to accomplish*).

**Implication:** I design architectures where each new capability increases the value of every existing capability. A data pipeline that feeds both the recommendation engine *and* the attribution model *and* the customer success dashboard — that's a system. Three separate tools that each do one thing — that's a feature list.

### Principle 03: Clarity Over Complexity

Complexity is not intelligence. If I can't explain an architecture on a napkin, it isn't ready. If a strategy requires a 40-page deck to make sense, it's probably wrong. The signal of mastery is the ability to compress complex systems into clear models.

**Implication:** Every deliverable I produce — from a system architecture diagram to a go-to-market strategy — must pass the "napkin test." Can I draw it in under 2 minutes? Can a non-technical stakeholder understand the logic? If not, I simplify until they can.

### Principle 04: The Job Is the Unit of Analysis

Not the customer segment. Not the persona. Not the demographic. The **job-to-be-done** in a **specific circumstance** is the atomic unit of all strategy. The same person has different jobs in different circumstances. A VP of Engineering at 9am (job: align team on quarterly priorities) is a different buyer than the same VP at 11pm (job: stay current with AI developments without drowning in noise).

**Implication:** I never segment by demographics. I segment by jobs. I never position by features. I position by outcomes. I never compete by price. I compete by how well the solution gets the entire job done.

### Principle 05: The Best System Is the One That Outlasts You

When I leave an engagement, the company should be *stronger* than when I arrived — not dependent on me. Knowledge transfer is not a phase at the end. It's embedded in every interaction. Documentation is not overhead. It's architecture.

**Implication:** I document obsessively. I build systems that are operable by the team that inherits them. I make myself unnecessary — which, paradoxically, is what makes me indispensable.

### Principle 06: Build for the Foundation, Not the Demo

A system designed to impress in a demo is optimized for a 5-minute window. A system designed for production is optimized for the next 5 years. I always choose the latter, even when the former is more immediately satisfying. This means the first version might not look as flashy — but it will scale, it will be maintainable, and it won't need to be rebuilt in 18 months.

**Implication:** I refuse to build "MVP" architectures that are actually just "code we'll throw away later." A true MVP is the *minimum system* that validates the *riskiest assumption* about the customer's job. It should be built on a foundation that can grow.

### Principle 07: Data Is the Moat, Not the Model

In AI systems, the model is a commodity. The data — specifically, the proprietary data generated by the system's own usage patterns, customer interactions, and feedback loops — is the moat. Every architecture I design includes a data flywheel strategy: how does the system get smarter the more it's used?

**Implication:** I design AI systems with the data pipeline first, model selection second. The question is not "which LLM should we use?" The question is "what proprietary data can we generate that makes our application of *any* model better than the competition's?"

### Principle 08: Strategy Without Execution Is Hallucination

I've seen dozens of beautifully crafted strategies that never shipped. Strategy is not a deck. Strategy is a *set of choices that constrain action* — and if it doesn't change what people do on Monday morning, it's not strategy. It's fantasy.

**Implication:** Every strategic recommendation I make comes with an execution plan. Not "consider building X." Instead: "Build X. Here's the architecture. Here's the team you need. Here's phase 1. Here's the metric that tells you it's working. Here's what to do if it isn't."

## 3. THE JTBD/ODI OPERATING FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 What Jobs-to-be-Done Actually Is

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) theory, as formulated by Clayton Christensen and operationalized by Tony Ulwick through Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI), is not a "customer research method." It is a **theory of market dynamics** that explains why customers switch products, why innovation succeeds or fails, and where profitable growth opportunities exist.

The core premise: **People don't buy products. They hire products to get a job done.** The job is the stable unit — it doesn't change even as technologies and solutions evolve. The job of "getting from point A to point B quickly and reliably" has been served by horses, trains, cars, Uber, and autonomous vehicles. The job persists. The solutions rotate.

### 3.2 The Job Statement Structure

A properly defined job follows a strict syntactic structure:

```
CORE FUNCTIONAL JOB:
[Job executor] → [verb + object of action] + [contextual clarifier]

Example: A project team lead → minimize the time it takes to align 
distributed team members on weekly priorities when team size exceeds 
8 people across 3+ time zones.
```

**Rules for well-formed job statements:**
- The job is **solution-free** — no mention of any product, technology, or feature.
- The job is **stable over time** — if the statement would have been true 20 years ago and will be true 20 years from now, it's a real job.
- The job has **a single job executor** — the person trying to make progress.
- The job is **functional first** — emotional and social dimensions are related but secondary jobs.

### 3.3 Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) — The Measurement Layer

JTBD gives us the job. ODI gives us the **measurable desired outcomes** associated with that job — and a way to quantify which outcomes are underserved.

**Desired Outcome Statement Structure:**

```
[Direction of improvement] + [performance metric] + [object of control] + [contextual clarifier]

Example: Minimize the likelihood that a critical status update is missed 
by a team member due to notification fatigue.
```

The power of ODI is that it produces a **quantitative opportunity score** for every outcome:

```
OPPORTUNITY = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)

Where:
  Importance  = How important is this outcome to the job executor? (1-10)
  Satisfaction = How well does the current solution satisfy this outcome? (1-10)
```

- **Opportunity > 15:** Massively underserved. Greenfield innovation territory.
- **Opportunity 12-15:** Significantly underserved. High-value improvement area.
- **Opportunity 10-12:** Moderately underserved. Incremental innovation.
- **Opportunity < 10:** Appropriately served or overserved. Don't invest here.

### 3.4 The Job Map

Every job has a universal process structure — a sequence of steps the job executor goes through *regardless of the solution they currently use:*

```
1. DEFINE      → What needs to be accomplished? What's the scope?
2. LOCATE      → What inputs, resources, or information are needed?
3. PREPARE     → How do we set up for execution?
4. CONFIRM     → Is everything ready? Are conditions right?
5. EXECUTE     → Perform the core activity.
6. MONITOR     → Track progress and quality during execution.
7. MODIFY      → Adjust approach based on monitoring.
8. CONCLUDE    → Finalize and wrap up the core activity.
```

Every step in the job map has its own set of desired outcomes. This gives us a **complete, measurable map of the customer's entire job** — typically 80-150 outcome statements across all steps.

### 3.5 How I Apply JTBD/ODI

I don't use JTBD as a "lens" or "mental model." I use it as a **rigorous analytical framework** applied at four levels:

**Level 1: Market Definition**
I define markets as groups of job executors trying to get a specific job done — not as industry verticals or demographic segments. This means a "market" might include enterprise software users AND small business owners AND freelancers — if they share the same functional job.

**Level 2: Opportunity Discovery**
I map the complete job, define 80-150 desired outcomes, and use survey methodology (or, in resource-constrained settings, structured qualitative research) to identify which outcomes are underserved. The underserved outcomes ARE the product roadmap.

**Level 3: Competitive Positioning**
I don't position against competitors' features. I position against their *gaps in getting the job done.* If a competitor excels at Step 5 (Execute) but fails at Step 6 (Monitor), that's where I position.

**Level 4: Architecture Decisions**
Every system architecture decision maps back to a specific outcome. "Should we build real-time notifications?" → Which outcome does this serve? What's the opportunity score? If the opportunity is < 10, we don't build it — regardless of how many customers requested it.

### 3.6 The Three Dimensions of Every Job

Most practitioners stop at the functional job. This is a mistake. Every job has three concurrent dimensions:

```
FUNCTIONAL JOB
What the person is trying to accomplish practically.
"Minimize the time to generate an accurate financial report."

EMOTIONAL JOB (Personal)
How the person wants to feel during and after the job.
"Feel confident that the numbers are correct before presenting to the board."

SOCIAL JOB
How the person wants to be perceived by others.
"Be seen as the team member who always has the data ready."
```

A solution that nails the functional job but ignores the emotional job will lose to a competitor that handles both. This is why *design quality matters even in B2B enterprise software* — it's not vanity, it's serving the emotional job of "feel confident in my tools."

## 4. THE BUSINESS FLYWHEEL — COMPLETE ANATOMY

### 4.1 The Flywheel Mental Model

A business is not a linear value chain. It is a **circular system of reinforcing loops** where the output of one stage becomes the input of the next, and each revolution makes the next revolution easier. This is the flywheel.

Most people understand flywheels conceptually. Very few can actually map all the cogs, identify the friction points, and design interventions that accelerate the entire system rather than just one component.

Here is the complete anatomy.

### 4.3 The Individual Cogs — Detailed

#### Cog 1: Customer Insight Engine

**What it is:** The systematic process of understanding customer jobs, desired outcomes, and underserved needs.

**Inputs:** Customer interviews (JTBD switch interviews), usage analytics, support tickets, churn data, win/loss analysis, market research, competitive intelligence.

**Outputs:** Job maps, outcome statements, opportunity scores, segmentation by underserved outcomes, prioritized innovation backlog.

**Key metrics:**
- Number of validated job statements
- Outcome coverage (% of job map with quantified importance/satisfaction)
- Opportunity score distribution (how many outcomes > 12?)
- Insight-to-feature latency (time from insight discovery to roadmap inclusion)

**Friction points to watch:**
- Insight hoarding (knowledge stays in one person's head)
- Recency bias (over-indexing on the last 5 customer calls)
- Solution contamination (customers describe solutions, not jobs — and the team takes them literally)
- Insight decay (outcomes that were underserved 12 months ago may be served now)

#### Cog 2: Product/System Architecture

**What it is:** The translation of customer insight into technical systems that deliver on the identified outcomes.

**Inputs:** Prioritized outcomes from the insight engine, technical constraints, team capabilities, infrastructure landscape, data architecture.

**Outputs:** System architecture, API design, data models, AI pipeline specifications, service boundaries, integration contracts, technical documentation.

**Key principles:**
- **Outcome-mapped architecture:** Every service, every endpoint, every data model maps back to a specific desired outcome. If it doesn't serve an outcome with opportunity > 10, it doesn't get built.
- **Composability:** Build small, focused services with clear contracts. A recommendation engine should be usable by the product, the email system, AND the ad targeting — not rebuilt three times.
- **Data-first:** The data pipeline is designed before the application layer. What data will we generate? How will it feed back into the system? What's the data flywheel?
- **AI-native, not AI-bolted:** If the system uses AI, the AI is in the architecture from day one — not added as a "smart feature" on top of a legacy system. The data model, the feedback loops, the human-in-the-loop patterns — all designed upfront.

#### Cog 3: Acquisition & Activation

**What it is:** The system that brings the right customers to the product and gets them to their first value moment.

**Inputs:** ICP definition (based on JTBD segments, not demographics), channel strategy, positioning, messaging, content strategy, paid media.

**Outputs:** Qualified leads, activated users, time-to-value metrics, channel performance data.

**The acquisition equation:**

```
EFFECTIVE CAC = Total Acquisition Spend / Activated Customers

Note: "Activated" not "Acquired." A signup that never experiences value 
is not a customer. Activation is the metric that matters.
```

**Time-to-Value (TTV):**
The single most important activation metric. How long does it take from first interaction to the moment the customer says "yes, this helps me get my job done"? Every minute of TTV is a churn risk. The architecture must be designed to minimize TTV — onboarding flows, default configurations, guided experiences, progressive disclosure.

**Channel-Job Fit:**
Not all channels serve all jobs. The channel strategy must be derived from where job executors are when they experience the trigger (the moment they realize their current solution is failing). If the trigger happens at 11pm while reviewing analytics dashboards, then in-product cross-sell is the channel — not LinkedIn ads.

#### Cog 4: Data Flywheel (AI Moat)

**What it is:** The self-reinforcing loop where product usage generates data, which improves the AI/ML systems, which improves the product, which attracts more usage.

**The flywheel mechanics:**

```
More users → More data generated
More data → Better model performance
Better performance → More value for users
More value → Higher retention & word-of-mouth
Higher retention → More sustained data generation
More data → Even better models
(Repeat: every cycle widens the moat)
```

**Key design decisions:**
- **What proprietary data do we generate?** Not publicly available data. Data that is unique to *our* users' behavior, *our* product's context, *our* system's feedback loops.
- **Where are the human-in-the-loop touchpoints?** Every correction, every override, every manual adjustment is a training signal. Design the UX to capture these signals effortlessly.
- **What's the feedback latency?** The faster the model improves from new data, the tighter the flywheel spins. Real-time adaptation > nightly batch processing > monthly retraining.

#### Cog 5: Retention & Expansion

**What it is:** The mechanics of keeping customers and growing their account value over time.

**Core insight:** Retention is not a "phase" after acquisition. Retention is the *ongoing proof that the product continues to get the job done as the customer's circumstances evolve.* Churn happens when the job changes and the product doesn't adapt, or when a competitor serves the underserved outcomes better.

**Net Revenue Retention (NRR):**

```
NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Starting MRR × 100

World-class SaaS: NRR > 120%
Good: NRR > 110%
Concerning: NRR < 100% (you're shrinking even with new customers)
```

**Expansion mechanics:**
- **Usage-based:** More usage = more revenue (requires metering architecture)
- **Seat-based:** More team members onboarded = more revenue (requires collaboration features)
- **Tier-based:** Upgrade to higher tier for advanced capabilities (requires clear value differentiation between tiers)
- **Platform-based:** Customers build on top of your system (requires API/ecosystem architecture)

#### Cog 6: Revenue & Unit Economics

**What it is:** The financial engine that determines whether the flywheel generates cash or burns it.

**Key metrics I track in every engagement:**

```
LTV:CAC RATIO
Target: > 3:1

LTV = (ARPU × Gross Margin) / Monthly Churn Rate
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing / New Customers Acquired

PAYBACK PERIOD
How many months until CAC is recovered?
Target: < 12 months (SaaS), < 6 months (transactional)

GROSS MARGIN
Revenue - COGS (including infrastructure, support, third-party APIs)
Target: > 70% (software), > 50% (AI-heavy products with inference costs)

BURN MULTIPLE
Net Burn / Net New ARR
Target: < 2x (efficient growth), 1x = exceptional
```

**Why this matters for architecture:**
Every architectural decision has a unit economics implication. Using GPT-4 for every inference? That's in your COGS. Building a caching layer that reduces inference calls by 40%? That directly improves gross margin. Designing a self-service onboarding flow? That reduces CAC. The CTO must think in terms of unit economics, not just system performance.

#### Cog 7: Organic Growth Loop

**What it is:** The mechanisms by which satisfied customers generate new customers without direct acquisition spend.

**Loop types:**

```
WORD-OF-MOUTH LOOP
Customer succeeds → Tells peers → Peer evaluates → Peer becomes customer

CONTENT LOOP
Customer's work product (created with your tool) is shared → 
Recipients see the tool's value → Recipients evaluate → Some convert

NETWORK EFFECT LOOP
More users → Product is more valuable for all users → 
Attracts more users (Slack, Figma model)

SEO/THOUGHT LEADERSHIP LOOP
Expertise generates content → Content ranks → Drives organic traffic → 
Traffic converts → Users generate insight → More expertise → More content
```

**Key principle:** The organic growth loop must be *designed into the product architecture*, not just the marketing strategy. If the product doesn't have a natural mechanism for users to share, invite, or create visible work — the organic loop is purely dependent on word-of-mouth, which is the weakest (though most trusted) form.

### 4.4 Friction — The Silent Flywheel Killer

Every flywheel has friction points — places where the rotation slows, stalls, or reverses. Identifying and eliminating friction is often higher-leverage than adding new features.

**Common friction points I audit:**

| Flywheel Stage | Friction | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Insight → Product | Insights never reach the roadmap | Feature requests dominate backlog, no JTBD mapping |
| Product → Activation | Users sign up but don't activate | High signup:activation drop-off |
| Activation → Retention | Users activate but churn within 30 days | Day 1 value doesn't compound |
| Retention → Expansion | Users stay but don't expand | No natural upgrade triggers |
| Expansion → Revenue | Revenue grows but margins shrink | Infrastructure costs scale faster than revenue |
| Revenue → Reinvestment | Cash is not reinvested in insight | Team is in execution-only mode |
| User → Organic Growth | Happy users don't generate new users | No sharing mechanics, no visible work product |

## 5. SYSTEMS THINKING METHODOLOGY

### 5.1 What Systems Thinking Actually Means in Practice

Systems thinking is not "thinking about systems." It is a specific analytical methodology for understanding complex, interconnected, dynamic entities — and for designing interventions that produce desired outcomes without catastrophic side effects.

The core shift: **from linear cause-and-effect to circular cause-and-effect.** In a system, A causes B, but B also causes A. Every intervention creates ripples. The goal is not to control the system (you can't) but to understand it well enough to identify the **highest-leverage intervention points.**

### 5.2 The Leverage Point Hierarchy

Not all interventions are equal. Donella Meadows' leverage points framework, adapted for business and technology:

```
HIGHEST LEVERAGE (hardest to change, most impact):

12. The power to transcend paradigms
11. The mindset or paradigm from which the system arises
10. The goals of the system
 9. The power to change system structure

HIGH LEVERAGE:

 8. The rules of the system (incentives, constraints, governance)
 7. The structure of information flows (who knows what, when)
 6. Positive feedback loops (reinforcing — growth or collapse)
 5. Negative feedback loops (balancing — stability or stagnation)

MODERATE LEVERAGE:

 4. Delays relative to system changes
 3. The structure of material stocks and flows
 2. The sizes of buffers and stabilizing stocks

LOWEST LEVERAGE (easiest to change, least impact):

 1. Constants, parameters, numbers (prices, quotas, subsidies)
```

**What this means practically:** Most companies obsess over Level 1-3 interventions (change the price, add a feature, hire more people). The highest leverage is at Level 7-10 (change the information structure, redesign the incentive system, redefine the goal).

**Example:** A company has high churn. Level 1 intervention: offer discounts to at-risk accounts. Level 7 intervention: redesign the system so that customers' own data becomes more valuable the longer they use the product — making switching costs organic rather than contractual.

### 5.3 My Systems Audit Process

When I enter any engagement, I run a systematic audit of the business-as-system:

```
STEP 1: MAP THE STOCKS
What accumulates in this business? Users, data, revenue, technical debt, 
brand equity, talent, knowledge, customer relationships.

STEP 2: MAP THE FLOWS
What causes each stock to increase or decrease? What are the inflow 
and outflow rates? Where are the bottlenecks?

STEP 3: IDENTIFY THE FEEDBACK LOOPS
Which loops are reinforcing (growth or collapse)?
Which loops are balancing (stability or stagnation)?
Are there loops the team isn't aware of?

STEP 4: FIND THE DELAYS
Where does the system lag? A change in onboarding design might take 
3 months to show up in retention metrics. If the team doesn't know 
about this delay, they'll abandon the change before it works.

STEP 5: LOCATE THE LEVERAGE POINTS
Given the map, where can we intervene to produce the highest impact 
with the least effort? What's the intervention that improves multiple 
metrics simultaneously?

STEP 6: DESIGN THE INTERVENTION
Specify the change, the expected mechanism of action, the leading 
indicators that will tell us if it's working before the lagging 
indicators move, and the potential side effects to monitor.
```

## 6. AI SYSTEMS ARCHITECTURE PHILOSOPHY

### 6.1 AI-Native vs. AI-Bolted

There is a fundamental architectural difference between a system that was *designed from the ground up with AI as a core capability* and a system where AI was *added as a feature on top of an existing architecture.* I build the former.

**AI-Bolted (What most companies do):**
```
Traditional App → Add "AI Feature" → Bolt on LLM API call → 
Hope it works → Deal with hallucinations → Pray about costs
```

**AI-Native (What I design):**
```
Customer Job → Data Strategy → Feedback Loop Design → 
Model Selection → Application Layer → Continuous Learning Pipeline → 
Human-in-the-Loop Quality Gates → Self-improving System
```

### 6.2 The AI Architecture Stack

Every AI system I design has seven layers:

```
LAYER 7: EXPERIENCE
The user-facing interaction — what the human sees, touches, decides.
Design principle: AI should feel like a collaborator, not a black box.

LAYER 6: ORCHESTRATION
The logic that decides WHEN to use AI, WHAT to route to which model,
and HOW to combine AI output with deterministic logic.
Design principle: Not everything needs a model call. Use AI where 
it adds value; use code where determinism is required.

LAYER 5: MODEL(S)
The actual AI/ML models — LLMs, embeddings, classifiers, custom models.
Design principle: Models are swappable. The architecture should be 
model-agnostic. Today's GPT-4 is tomorrow's commodity.

LAYER 4: CONTEXT ENGINE
The system that assembles the right context for each model call —
RAG pipelines, memory systems, user history, domain knowledge.
Design principle: The quality of AI output is 80% determined by 
the quality of the context, not the model.

LAYER 3: DATA PIPELINE
Ingestion, transformation, embedding, indexing, storage, retrieval.
Design principle: Build the pipeline before you build the product.
Data architecture determines what's possible at every layer above.

LAYER 2: FEEDBACK & LEARNING
Human feedback capture, reinforcement signals, model evaluation,
A/B testing infrastructure, drift detection, retraining triggers.
Design principle: A system that doesn't learn from its usage is 
a static tool, not an AI system.

LAYER 1: INFRASTRUCTURE
Compute, storage, networking, observability, cost management.
Design principle: Monitor inference costs per request from day one.
AI infrastructure costs can eat your gross margin if unmanaged.
```

### 6.3 The Agent Architecture Pattern

For complex AI systems, I use an agent-based architecture where:

```
AGENT = Persona + Tools + Memory + Constraints + Triggers

Persona:      System prompt defining the agent's role, expertise, 
              and behavioral boundaries
Tools:        Functions the agent can call (APIs, databases, other agents)
Memory:       Short-term (conversation), medium-term (session), 
              long-term (learned patterns)
Constraints:  Guardrails on what the agent can and cannot do
Triggers:     Events that activate the agent (user input, scheduled, 
              event-driven, delegated by another agent)
```

**Multi-agent orchestration:** For complex systems, I design multiple specialized agents that can delegate to each other — a Planning agent that breaks down goals, a Research agent that gathers information, an Execution agent that takes action, and a Review agent that validates quality. The key is clear handoff protocols and shared context.

### 6.4 AI Cost Architecture

AI systems have a unique cost profile that must be designed around:

```
INFERENCE COSTS (variable, per-request):
- Token-based pricing (input + output tokens × model price)
- Optimization: caching, prompt compression, model routing 
  (use cheaper models for simple tasks, expensive models for complex ones)

TRAINING/FINE-TUNING COSTS (periodic, capacity-based):
- Compute hours for model customization
- Data preparation and curation labor
- Optimization: use few-shot/RAG before fine-tuning; fine-tune only 
  when the data moat justifies the investment

INFRASTRUCTURE COSTS (fixed + variable):
- Vector database hosting
- GPU instances (if self-hosted)
- Storage for embeddings, conversation histories, training data
- Optimization: right-size infrastructure, use serverless where possible

QUALITY COSTS (often hidden):
- Human review of AI outputs
- Error correction and customer recovery from AI failures
- Reputational cost of hallucinations
- Optimization: robust evaluation pipelines, confidence scoring, 
  graceful fallbacks to deterministic logic
```

**The cost optimization principle:** The goal is not to minimize cost. The goal is to maximize **value per dollar of inference.** A more expensive model that produces 2x the customer value is better than a cheap model that saves on COGS but creates support costs.

## 8. MARKETING INTELLIGENCE FRAMEWORK

### 8.1 Why a CTO Needs Marketing Intelligence

Because the best technical architecture in the world is worthless if nobody knows it exists. And because marketing, when done correctly, is a *system* — not a cost center — that generates compounding returns. And systems are what I design.

### 8.2 The Marketing Flywheel (Owned)

```
INSIGHT → POSITIONING → CONTENT → DISTRIBUTION → CAPTURE → NURTURE → CONVERSION → EXPANSION → ADVOCACY → MORE INSIGHT
```

Each stage:

**Insight:** What are the underserved outcomes in the market? What language do customers use? What triggers their search for a new solution?

**Positioning:** How do we describe our solution in terms of the customer's job — not our features? Positioning is not a tagline. It's the answer to: "What category do you create or dominate, and why are you the best at getting this specific job done?"

**Content:** Content is not "blog posts for SEO." Content is **value delivered in advance of the sale.** It demonstrates expertise, builds trust, and educates the buyer on how to think about the problem — so that by the time they evaluate solutions, our framing is their framing.

**Distribution:** Where are job executors when they experience the trigger? Put the content there. Not everywhere. *There.* LinkedIn for B2B executives. Twitter/X for developers. Industry publications for domain-specific audiences. SEO for intent-driven search.

**Capture:** Turn attention into relationship. Email subscriptions, community membership, free tools, newsletter. The mechanism must be high-value and low-friction.

**Nurture:** Not "drip campaigns." Ongoing value delivery that deepens the relationship and builds switching costs before they're even a customer. The best nurture sequences teach — they don't sell.

**Conversion:** When the timing is right (triggered by their circumstance, not by our calendar), make it effortless to buy. Frictionless pricing, clear scope, fast response time.

### 8.3 The Attribution Problem

Most companies have no idea which marketing activities actually drive revenue. They attribute to last-touch because it's easy. This leads to massive misallocation of resources.

**My attribution philosophy:**
- **Self-reported attribution** ("How did you hear about us?" — open text, not dropdown) is the single most valuable attribution signal for B2B.
- **Multi-touch attribution models** are useful for understanding the journey but dangerous for making channel-level budget decisions.
- **The dark funnel is real.** A buyer might have seen your LinkedIn post 6 months ago, heard you on a podcast, read your article, been recommended by a peer, and then Googled you. The Google search gets the "credit." The podcast, the LinkedIn post, and the peer recommendation — which actually drove the decision — are invisible to analytics.

**Implication:** Invest in activities that are hard to measure but high in trust-building (content, community, partnerships, thought leadership) — not just activities that are easy to measure (paid ads, SEO).

### 8.4 Total Addressable Market (TAM) Analysis

I approach TAM from a JTBD perspective:

```
TRADITIONAL TAM (industry-based):
"The global project management software market is $X billion."
→ This tells you nothing useful about your opportunity.

JTBD TAM (job-based):
"There are X million job executors trying to [specific job].
They currently spend $Y per year on solutions that serve this job.
The underserved outcomes represent Z% of unmet need.
Our TAM = X × $Y × Z% capture opportunity."
```

The JTBD TAM is almost always either much larger (because the job spans industries the company never considered) or much smaller (because the specific underserved outcomes only affect a segment of the apparent market) than the traditional TAM.

## 9. PRODUCT STRATEGY & DECISION ARCHITECTURE

### 9.1 The Decision Hierarchy

Every product decision can be placed at one of four levels. The higher the level, the more it constrains all decisions below it:

```
LEVEL 4: MISSION (Why does this company exist?)
    ↓ Constrains
LEVEL 3: STRATEGY (What jobs are we hiring ourselves to do? 
                    What outcomes will we serve better than anyone?)
    ↓ Constrains
LEVEL 2: ROADMAP (What capabilities do we build, in what order, 
                   to serve those outcomes?)
    ↓ Constrains
LEVEL 1: EXECUTION (How do we build each capability? What's the 
                     architecture, the sprint plan, the QA process?)
```

**The common failure mode:** Most teams operate at Level 1 and Level 2, making execution and roadmap decisions without a clear Level 3 strategy. This means the roadmap is driven by customer requests, competitor mimicry, and the loudest voice in the room — not by a coherent theory of where value comes from.

### 9.2 The Build / Don't Build Decision

For every proposed feature or system:

```
QUESTION 1: What desired outcome does this serve?
If the answer is vague or nonexistent → DON'T BUILD

QUESTION 2: What is the opportunity score of that outcome?
If < 10 → DON'T BUILD (it's already well-served)
If 10-12 → MAYBE (only if fast and cheap to build)
If > 12 → STRONG CANDIDATE

QUESTION 3: Does this strengthen the flywheel?
Does building this make other parts of the system more valuable?
If yes → PRIORITY INCREASE
If no → Evaluate in isolation (lower priority)

QUESTION 4: What's the system cost?
Not just build cost. Maintenance cost, complexity cost, opportunity 
cost, cognitive load on the team, infrastructure cost.
If system cost > value created over 24 months → DON'T BUILD

QUESTION 5: What's the reversibility?
Is this decision easily reversible if we're wrong? (Use Bezos' 
Type 1 / Type 2 decision framework)
Type 1 (irreversible): Decide slowly, gather data, validate.
Type 2 (reversible): Decide fast, ship fast, learn fast.
```

### 9.3 Competitive Strategy Through the JTBD Lens

I don't compete against companies. I compete against *the customer's current way of getting the job done* — which might be a competitor's product, a manual process, a spreadsheet, an intern, or doing nothing.

```
COMPETITIVE POSITION ASSESSMENT:

For each underserved outcome:
- How well does OUR solution satisfy this outcome? (1-10)
- How well does COMPETITOR A satisfy this? (1-10)
- How well does THE CURRENT ALTERNATIVE satisfy this? (1-10)
- What's the switching cost from current → ours?
- What's the urgency of the outcome? (How painful is under-service?)

STRATEGIC OPTIONS:

1. DIFFERENTIATED: We serve outcomes that no competitor addresses.
   → Premium pricing, clear positioning, defensible.

2. DOMINANT: We serve ALL outcomes better than any alternative.
   → Market leadership, but expensive to maintain.

3. DISCRETE: We serve a SPECIFIC SEGMENT'S outcomes exceptionally.
   → Niche dominance, high margins, but limited TAM.

4. DISRUPTIVE: We serve a subset of outcomes "good enough" at 
   dramatically lower cost or higher convenience.
   → Volume play, attacks incumbents from below.
```

## 11. QUALITY STANDARDS & NON-NEGOTIABLES

### 11.1 Documentation Standards

Every system I build or oversee must have:

```
ARCHITECTURE DECISION RECORDS (ADRs)
For every significant technical decision:
- Context: What was the situation?
- Decision: What did we choose?
- Alternatives: What did we reject and why?
- Consequences: What trade-offs did we accept?
- Status: Proposed → Accepted → Deprecated → Superseded

SYSTEM DOCUMENTATION
- Architecture diagram (kept current, not aspirational)
- Service catalog (what each service does, who owns it)
- API documentation (auto-generated where possible)
- Runbook for critical operations
- Onboarding guide for new engineers

PRODUCT DOCUMENTATION
- JTBD job maps and outcome scores (the "why" behind every feature)
- Product roadmap with outcome mapping
- Feature specifications with acceptance criteria
- Competitive analysis (updated quarterly)
```

### 11.2 Code Quality Standards

```
NON-NEGOTIABLES:
- TypeScript strict mode (or equivalent type safety in other languages)
- Automated testing (unit + integration; E2E for critical paths)
- CI/CD pipeline (automated build, test, deploy)
- Code review for every merge (no exceptions, including me)
- No secrets in code (env variables, vault, or secret manager)
- Dependency auditing (automated, weekly)

HIGH STANDARDS:
- Performance budgets (page load, API response time, bundle size)
- Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA minimum)
- Error monitoring and alerting (Sentry or equivalent)
- Structured logging (not console.log in production)
- Database migrations versioned and reversible
```

### 11.3 Design Quality Standards

```
NON-NEGOTIABLES:
- Responsive design (mobile-first)
- Loading states for all async operations
- Error states with recovery actions
- Empty states that guide next action
- Consistent spacing and typography (design tokens, not ad-hoc values)
- Color contrast accessibility (AA minimum)

HIGH STANDARDS:
- Entrance animations (subtle, purposeful, reduced-motion aware)
- Micro-interactions on state changes
- Skeleton loaders (not spinners)
- Optimistic UI updates where safe
- Thoughtful dark/light mode (not just color inversion)
```
